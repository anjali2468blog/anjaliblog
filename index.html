An Extended Definition: Power

Power is used in many contexts. It exists in politics, classrooms, workplaces, social circles, and even amidst conversations. But what does power really mean? As the Merriam-Webster dictionary states, is it just the possession of control, authority, or influence over others? Or is it something different depending on the context?

When speaking of power, we often associate it with politicians, CEOs and large corporations. But power is not reserved solely for those at the top. Power is generally about influencing resources, people, or decisions (Weber). Numerous intricate hierarchies control various things on campus premises, such as power dynamics between students and teachers and between teachers and faculty. According to Michael Neumann's article about the school power structure, power is relational and not just about titles.

In an academic setting, power is present in every aspect, with hierarchies being build up in smallest groups. Whether we are working on a group project or professor grading our paper, power exists in each and every activity. In this context, knowledge, confidence and interpersonal skills are power rather than concrete authority.

When talking about economic power, we do not need to look further than our tuition fees. Although the fees are affordable, students often struggle with economic power. Students with adequate financial resources have access to more opportunities and extracurricular activities in compared to other students.

Social hierarchies and stereotypes are present in all academic settings. It is about who can influence others and make their voice heard. According to sociologist Diana Kendal, social power is the ability to influence cultural norms and social interactions. This is visible by the students who dominate conversations and use social media to influence campus culture.

We have observed that during conversations, some voices are given more weight than others or presented with more opportunities. It may be due to their interpersonal skills or their ability to find possibilities amidst the challenges but that isn't always the case. Students are also categorized into different groups based on popularity, social status, economic status, and influence, creating a visible hierarchy among themselves. Sometimes, they are misunderstood and stereotyped, whereas sometimes, they create these labels themselves. Understanding these dynamics and breaking the stereotypes can contribute towards creating a more inclusive environment and better student resources.

Conclusion: Bringing it all Together.

Power is everywhere, within us as individuals, and it shapes how we learn and interact at the campus. From economic power that influences our financial decisions to social power that shapes campus culture to navigating the conversation according to your choice, we are surrounded by power everywhere. Power is a complex thing that lies in the gray area – its impact depends on how it is used. The question is, when you place yourself in a powerful position, how will you use it? Will you use it to exploit or use it for everyone's benefit?
